AI is reshaping work. But not evenly, and not all at once. How exposed your job is depends on what you do. How well you can weather a change depends on who you are and what you've built.

Understanding Adaptive Capacity

A 2026 study by Manning & Aguirre examined how different occupations would fare in an AI-driven labor market transition. They found that exposure to AI varies dramatically by occupation — but that exposure alone doesn’t determine outcomes.

What matters is adaptive capacity: a combination of financial resources, transferable skills, geographic labor market conditions, and career stage. Workers with high adaptive capacity can navigate transitions more effectively, even when their occupation faces significant AI exposure.

Researchers at Brookings measured the concept of “adaptive capacity” across 752 occupations. This unofficial tool asks: what is your adaptive capacity, specifically?

      Context & Caveats

      This tool is not a prediction of job loss, financial advice, or career counseling. It is a framework for thinking about readiness for labor market transitions, based on peer-reviewed research.

      Limitations: The study uses occupation-level averages; individual circumstances vary. The composite score combines occupation-level data with self-reported inputs using simplified assumptions about wealth and age distributions. Skill transferability is measured by O*NET skill profile similarity, which may not capture all relevant factors.

      Research: Manning & Aguirre (2026), "Who Bears the Costs of AI Displacement?" NBER Working Paper 34705. AI exposure scores from Eloundou et al. (2024), Science 384(6702).

      Data: O*NET 30.1, BLS Employment Projections 2024–2034, BLS OEWS May 2024, Census CBSA Gazetteer 2024. Built as a research translation project. Your data never leaves your browser.
